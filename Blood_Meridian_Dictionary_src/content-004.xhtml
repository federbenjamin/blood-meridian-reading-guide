 isomers

(noun) In chemistry, compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements; here used as an image for frost-coated shrubs that are ghostly mirror versions of their own shapes.

“shrubs were like polar isomers of their own shapes”

 jacal

(noun) A vernacular dwelling of the Texas–Mexico borderlands: vertical poles or timbers planted in the ground and lashed together, with gaps filled by wattle and mud plaster. From Nahuatl xacalli (“hut”). The most common rural housing form in South Texas and northern Mexico in the 19th century.

“a solitary jacal, crude hut of mud and wattles”

 jacales

(Spanish) Simple huts or shacks; from Nahuatl xacalli, a dwelling of branches, straw, or adobe, common in rural Mexico.

“past the first of a row of jacales”

 jackal wolves

(noun) An older colloquial name for coyotes (Canis latrans), which were also called “prairie wolves,” “brush wolves,” and “American jackals” by 19th-century settlers because of their resemblance to both jackals and small wolves.

“The little jackal wolves cried from across the river”

 jackboots

(noun) Tall, sturdy boots reaching to mid-calf or the knee, originally heavy military cavalry boots; here worn by the frontier dancers.

“the board floor slamming under the jackboots”

 jackhares

(noun) Jackrabbits — large North American hares (Lepus spp.) common in desert and plains regions. McCarthy uses an archaic compound form of the name.

“Mad jackhares started and checked in the blue glare”

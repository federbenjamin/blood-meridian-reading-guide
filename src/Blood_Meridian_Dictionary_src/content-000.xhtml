(noun) Here used in an astronomical or cosmological sense for the broad bands or zones of matter in the sky; not the common modern sense of a highway ring road.

“distant beltways of matter grinding mutely to the west”

 benches

(noun) Flat, shelf-like terraces of land on a hillside or mountainside, resembling steps.

“on those grassy benches the great shambling figures of bears”

 benchland

(noun) 1. A long, relatively flat strip of elevated ground bounded by steeper slopes above and below, typically a former floodplain left standing above a river’s current level by downward cutting.

“they rode along a benchland above the gaunt rill of water”

(noun) 2. A flat or gently sloping terrace of land partway up a canyon or hillside, formed by differential erosion and standing above the valley floor.

“When he reached the benchland above the canyon floor”

 benighted

(verb / adjective) Overtaken by darkness or nightfall while traveling; to be caught out in the night.

“darkness overtook them and they were benighted in that place”

 benjamin

(noun) 19th-century British and American slang for an overcoat or loose outer coat; the word derives, by biblical analogy with Joseph’s coat, from the name Benjamin, Joseph’s younger brother.

“an old woolen benjamin over the one shoulder”
